There is an emerging industry thought to be only science fiction not too long ago, that is fast becoming a reality: Space Tourism. The main supporting infrastructure for space tourism is a spaceport and the spaceport or cosmodrome is defined to be a    
        site for launching or receiving spacecraft. The cosmodrome has historically been used for sites capable of launching spacecraft into orbit around Earth or on interplanetary trajectories. However, launch sites for purely sub-orbital flights are increasingly    
        coined as spaceports.

        The future of Aviation is the Spaceport. As the world embraces this new frontier of travel, design solutions will be required to ensure it will not be at the expense of the environment. The re-adaptability of airports or aerodromes to support and integrate    
        spaceports will be essential as it is not only a sustainable approach to revolutionise travel, but it will also ensure that the built landscape will be rooted in the tenet of ambition and continuation. The spaceport has the potential to give the world a whole    
        new perspective to space tourism, by shaping future lifestyles, connect local and global communities to a mere stone throw away as well as meeting the core needs of convenience, personalisation, and connectivity that the world yearns for.

   Despite the Covid-19 crisis, the race to the commercial viability of spaceflights has increased over the last few years. In particular, the last few months saw Richard branson, the founder of virgin galactic and Jeff bezos, the founder of blue origin and amazon successfully flew to
   space in the spacecrafts developed by their respective companies.

   This will no doubt assure the general public and potential paying passengers of the reliability of spaceflights.

               THE PRIME SITE CHOICE: SINGAPORE


               The aviation industry in Singapore is one of the most integral components of the Singapore economy, and it is regarded as a major aviation hub in Southeast Asia, with Changi Airport consistently ranked as the sixth busiest
               airport in SEA. Additionally, Singapore’s location along the equator not only allows the spacecraft to carry lesser fuels to reach their desired location but also cost savings for commercial space operators.

               Finally, the island already boasts one of the premier seaports in the world, and the addition of a spaceport can propel Singapore as a major transportation hub for the sea, air, and space.

              In Singapore, there are 4 main airbase and an airport. On a micro scale, the airspace is segregated to no-fly zones which are restricted airspace to prevent aerial objects flown near aircrafts, which can endanger the lives of those on
              board and on the ground. Thus, these no-fly zones usually exists right above the airbase or airport and no aerial activities can be conducted in the vicinity without a permit.

              The study of the flight paths reveals that in general, military aircrafts land from the south to avoid airspace infringement in the neighbouring airspace. Due to the proximity of paya lebar airbase to changi airport, aircrafts cannot turn
              towards changi else it will interfere with the civil air traffic and cause a disruption. Helicopters mainly transit via the coastal areas of the island and a predefined flight path ensures minimal flight over buildings and residential estates to
              reduce noise pollution. The airstrip on Pulau Sudong, off the southern coast of Singapore is only utilised for emergencies involving military aircraft.

              The area of impact is a 3200m or 2-mile radius from the airfield which are hazard areas that could potentially be affected by debris from a launch or re entry accident. This study could then determine the selection of site that is most
              feasible for integrating a spaceport.

               These are the 5 main factors to select a site for a spaceport. The site must first support a runway that is at least or capable of extending to 3000m and a segregated airspace for spaceflights with the main priority of
               protecting the uninvolved public.

               The site is preferably located away from urban areas while relevant health and safety legislation should be in place such as the safe storage of hazardous fuels.

               Strong crosswinds on site could impede spaceflight operatives and commercial implications may result from poor weather, such as the experience of spaceflight not living up to the expectation of the passenger.

               In the short-term, aviation environmental regulations would apply to spaceplane operations while environmental issues and storage of hazardous materials like fuels need to be considered as well.

               Lastly, the site must have good surface access to link space tourists to the spaceport.

               One can imagine that in the near future, spaceports becoming centers of great economic activity, supporting passenger flights around the world, into orbit, or interplanetary flights. Mankind is reaching out to the stars,
               and spaceports are where the first steps to spaceflight are being taken. By fostering the development of commercial spaceports today, one can build a road to tomorrow.
