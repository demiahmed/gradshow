To mitigate urban heat island in existing cities through the tactical introduction of water cooling strategies for minimal disruption with maximal effect.

Urban Morphology

Urban morphology plays a crucial role in UHI due to its influence on wind porosity. This can be seen through the large amounts of research that has gone into studying urban morphology and prescribing urban design guidelines to counter UHI. These is usually in the form of building and urban typologies relating to building shape, size and placement. However, while these prescriptions are effective for the designing of new cities, they do little in the improvement of existing cities, where changes can be difficult and costly. Mitigation strategies should be focused on improving existing built up cities to be more resilient.

Urban morphology provides useful insight into the microclimatic conditions in relation to the built environment. Data such as Ground Coverage Ratio (GCR) can be calculated to visualise the urban density at a higher resolution than generic numerical data across regions, while Frontal Area Density (FAD) provides an estimation of the pedestrian level wind conditions. Climatic data extracted from official weather stations provides a way to validate GCR and FAD visualisations prior to identifying areas of interests in relation to UHI.

Site Selection

Most readily available maps display density in the form of the number of people within the districts. However, when considering urban morphology, building footprint would provide a better estimation of wind flow and thus, UHI intensity. Hence, GCR and FAD visualisations provide better representations of UHI by considering the building's footprint. For this study, the maps are computed into a 500m grid utilising GIS data.

Data Extraction & Validation

Using a self-written python script, the data can be extracted from the official API by Data.Gov and its raw data processed and evaluated into monthly and yearly breakdowns. For FAD validation, site specific wind rose diagrams are generated using Rhino-Grasshopper and appended onto the FAD map to rationalise if the wind flow is in coherent with its surrouding environment.

The weather stations in close proximity with the CBD area are selected for this validation.

Typology Study



Between the different building typologies amongst the tall skyscrapers in the district, cylindrical shaped buildings do not obstruct the wind flow as much as an angular building of the same frontal area density.

As such, a cylindrical building within a larger area of poor wind flow potential has the best potential to provide an area of effect and spread the benefits of evaporative cooling to a larger area.

The Brief


Design Proposal


In line with trying to fix the existing conditions, the design proposal would be in the form of A&A works, retrofitting the current building structure with evaporative cooling features and increasing porosity for naturally ventilated areas. The 4 features are:-
1) Water Wall,
2) Water Misting,
3) Rainwater Harvesting & Storage and
4) Adding Porosity

Water Wall

Experiment 1 - Surface Temperature


This experiment aims to provide insight into the thermal absorption properties of concrete, the primary heat trapping material in the CBD. Using thermocouple probes, the exterior and interior surface temperatures of a wall are monitored over a 24-hour duration. Both interior and exterior surface temperatures remained mostly above the recorded air temperature, and even continued increasing after the air temperature had peaked.

Experiment 2 - Concrete Thermal Absorption


The potential of material cooling via wetting is tested in the experiment. Using a 50W heating lamp, a casted block of concrete is warmed and its top and bottom surface temperatures recorded until it peaked. Then, the block is sprayed with water for 10 minutes while still being heated by the lamp, before it was left to be heated to its peak temperature again. This experiment showed that wetting the concrete not only had immediate cooling, but it also slowed down the concrete’s rate of heat absorption as well. A wetting cycle of 2.5 hours between each spray could be feasible.

Placement


The placement of the water walls would ideally be in the direction of higher solar irradiance. Based on the solar analysis using the Ladybug plugin in Rhino-Grasshopper, this shall be in the NNW and SSE directions of the building.

Water Misting

Experiment - Misting Effect


Using a garden spray fitted with a 0.15mm diameter nozzle, while sprayed over a fan shows feasible cooling over short distances.

Simulation - Misting Effect


Contextless misting simulations provides an optimal wind speed for evaporative cooling to be feasible. With this data, an array of misting systems can be located onto the site where the simulated wind speed is within the optimal range between 0.5/s to 2m/s.

Placement Process

1) Windflow is simulated for the street scale boundary
2) Using a C# written script in Rhino Grasshopper, regions with the optimal wind speed can be identified by points
3) Understanding that a misting system cannot be placed on the road, points within those regions are removed
4) Misting systems placed too far away from the site may become inefficient due to the loss of pressure associated with extended water transport distances. A 200m radius area of effect is identified
5) The points are grouped and enclosed within boundaries. If the boundary area is too small, it is removed
6) Placement points are scattered within the boundaries. The number of placements within each boundary is dependent on the area of boundary divided by an area of effect per misting system

Simulation

The simulation results showed slight decrease in air temperature. While the drop in temperature is not much, the simulation was done with only 20 misting systems across a 200m radius from the site. A larger number of misting systems would likely provide better cooling results. The misting systems are placed at a 4m height from the ground for efficient water dispersal (3m optimal) with reduced wetting sensation on pedestrians.

Building Porosity

Design


Redeveloping the building with residential units would make those floors naturally ventilated to comply with Fire Safety requirements. However, there has to be a balance between the porosity and number of units provided to ensure profitability of the building owner.

Simulation


The checkered typology provides the most amount of wind into the building interior spaces, but has the least number of units. The spiral typology provides the least wind obstruction, but with the least wind into the interior spaces. The double-spiral typology provides the best compromise between the checkered and the spiral.

Water Harvesting & Storage

Placement


The water wall and misting systems would require water to function. It would not be sustainable to use only fresh water for the system due to the increasing water scarcity and the substantial amount of energy required to pump the water. As such, rainwater harvesting could be implemented to provide the water required by the water wall and water mist systems, or at least reduce the amount of fresh water required. Harvesting and storing the rainwater higher up in the building allows for the passive movement of water by gravity, reducing the reliance on a pump. The calculation for the water required is a follow:-

Mass flow rate of 1 misting system = 6L/hour
Misting cycle = 10 min spray & 10 mins warming
Water usage = 2L/hour
Hours where cooling is required = 10 hours (9am – 7pm)
Water usage = 10L/day

2020 Annual precipitation = 1886.6mm (annual average is 2165.9mm)
Average Daily Precipitation = 1886.6/ 167 (number of rainy days) = 11.3mm or 11.3L/day/sqm

Wall Surface Area (180°) = 4067.8 sqm (residential floors)
Roof Area (minus 50% M&E) = 1165.7sqm
Total Surface Area = 5233.5 sqm
Total Surface Area (30% buffer) = 3663.5 sqm

Average rainy day water harvested = 41397.6L (41.4m3)

Proposed: 5 number of 2x2x2 water tanks

Design

To collect façade runoff, a deep gutter can be placed around the façade, draining the collected runoff into the collection tank located within the building. The tanks are placed within the core to reduce loading on the cantilevered slabs.
