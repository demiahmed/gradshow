Thus, to take advantage of these policies, the opportunity presents itself in repurposing car parks into urban agriculture spaces within Singapore’s public housing neighbourhoods, where 80% of the population reside. To achieve the “30 by 30” goal, up to 51 multi-storey car parks could be repurposed into commercial farming spaces. Considering that there are over 900 of these car parks around Singapore, 51 is a small fraction. So could we go further? The 2030 target is only a touchpoint, and more can be done in the following years to bolster Singapore’s food security.

A key feature of car parks is the gridded layout, whose dimensions are based on parking lot dimensions and driving clearances. This means that all HDB multi-storey car parks are built upon a similar grid system, advantageous for repeatable designs. Thus, a modular approach is favoured to ensure the same design approach can be used on different car parks. Solar analysis was conducted to understand annual rooftop solar irradiation, a key factor for agriculture. The southern half of the site receives less sunlight, due to the tall point blocks on either side. Thus, placing more growing space on the northern half would ensure crops can receive more sunlight. Using modules based on the grid system, a series of blocks were aggregated to form an initial farm massing. This height and depth ensures that crops can receive as much sunlight as possible, while also ensuring sufficient grow area to achieve production capacity.

To ensure a successful transition into sustainable community lifestyles, a three phased approach is proposed. The first phase sees the addition of the commercial farm, replacing the upper deck with commercial and public programmes. Food-oriented amenities such as farmer’s market, along with the aquaponics fish tanks and food packing are located below the rooftop level. The ground floor is use for F&B spaces and food waste systems. This forms the main hub, a seed planted within the community.

The second phase integrates the food hub with neighbouring blocks, by introducing community gardens and elevated linkways. Car parking is gradually reduced, creating more space for community programmes. By now, the establishment of the commercial farm in Phase 1 would ensure a wealth of contextual farming knowledge, to be passed onto the community. This availability of expertise and proximity to garden space hopes to cultivate awareness of food production.

The last phase looks at how individual households can take up their own urban agriculture practices, by encouraging household gardening. By phasing this last, residents would have gained a deeper appreciation for the natural systems behind what they eat, maybe even grown up with it being the norm. This encourages them to make sustainable lifestyle choices, the last step in long-term food security.

Circular systems are seen throughout the design concept. Water recycling and food production cycle are key in ensuring waste is minimized and allows the food hub to contribute back to the community. Harvested rainwater is used for crops, cleaned via the aquaponics systems, and recirculated through the building. Vegetable produce is distributed to the farmer’s market and F&B outlets, and the resulting food waste is broken down in the digester, along with food waste from the surrounding HDB blocks. This converts it into compost, used for mushroom cultivation. Leftover mushroom substrate is mixed with compost to fertilize crops.

The repurposing of HDB multi-storey car parks also demonstrates circularity, taking advantage of existing infrastructure and extending the lifecycle of the building. Using a kit of parts for programmatic layouts also ensures spaces can be easily fabricated and transported to various sites, with the potential to be plug-and-play, or even be able to hot-swap across various food hubs.




In Summary

AGROPOLIS envisions a decentralised network of food hubs throughout Singapore’s public housing estates, built upon existing multistorey HDB car parks. The integration food production with public spaces, taking advantage of car-lite initiatives and adaptive reuse of existing infrastructure, allows for a compact layout which can be replicated across multiple neighbourhoods with minimal redesign.

Circular systems is demonstrated on multiple levels of architectural design. Rainwater harvesting and water recycling, food waste collection for composting, and localised knowledge loops from commercial farm to residents minimize energy and resource consumption. A modular kit of parts and repurposing of the car park reduces construction and material costs.

Ultimately, this thesis aims to strengthen Singapore’s food security and connect communities with food production. This is achieved through normalising local produce through close proximity to the farming process, exposure to community-led gardens, and phased expansion of farm initiatives. This encourages communities to have a sense of ownership over local food production and cultivates long-term sustainable lifestyles. While this thesis speculates the possibility of neighbourhood food hubs, the architectural and engineering technologies already exist in some form. Through enhanced green policies and strengthened ties between government, industry and communities, the seed has been sown for a more sustainable tomorrow.
