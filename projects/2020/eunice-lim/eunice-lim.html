Modus Operandi: Next Generation Housing Methodology

Summary

The thesis aims to develop a new design methodology for housing projects of the future, where homes are flexible and adaptive to the occupants’ needs at different phases of their lives. The generic system will allow for the expansion and contraction of housing units to provide for a wide range of living options in the housing block, catering to the different and changing needs of the residents.

Current housing models usually take on a ‘cookie cutter’ approach, where standard unit designs are repeatedly stacked to form a tower, and they often lack the flexibility and personalization for the respective demographics in the housing market, apart from nuclear families. The thesis pushes for future housing models to take on a livelier and more dynamic design approach, where the liberty to customize one’s home provides for both the user’s needs, as well as the overall expression of the housing block.

The intention of designing a flexible habitation system is also to extend the overall life span of housing projects, allowing it to stay relevant to its users over an extended period. This is also in hopes to reduce the need for frequent building demolition and reconstruction, reducing the environmental impact caused by the built industry in the long run. Furthermore, this housing strategy will also tackle the issue of affordability, by allowing people to purchase for just the right amount of space their budget can afford. As the household financial circumstance changes over time, they can then subsequently opt to expand or contract their unit accordingly, without having to look for an alternative flat. This allows more financial flexibility for the residents and reduces their financial load on owning a property.

03// New Housing Design Methodology Proposal

The proposed new housing design methodology consists of 3 segments. Within each segment, the designers are able to loop through the steps multiple times before finalising on the details and move on to the next segment. The diagram below shows the design methodology that has been developed, and a step-by-step example of how it can be applied. The development of the new design methodology stems from the lessons learnt in the research and design study done in the stages of the thesis, for which will be explained in the later half of this page.

In addition to providing the methods for designing the building at its first built stage, the methodology also proposes various design evolution strategies, for which can take place during the main building transformation stages, set at 10 years interval. This is to ensure the building can address the needs for the future generations within the same housing building, extending the building life and reducing the need for families to look for alternative housing options.



Design Evolution Strategy 1 -
Interchangeable Spaces Within Unit
The first strategy is targeted at creating flexibility for interchangeable spaces within the unit. From the diagrams, we can see how the individual unit is customised at point of sale and built in year 2030 in stage 10 of the building design process. The most important feature is the layering in the function of the space, where the inner most region of the unit will be saved aside for utilities. The regions closer to the façade will constitute spaces which are interchangeable and have varied functions. The addition of external structures will be done through customised light-weight modules.


Design Evolution Strategy 2 -
Reconfiguring Spaces Between Units
The second design evolution strategy relates to how the reconfiguration between units can be done, specifically in the expansion or contraction of the units. As mentioned under the economical decisions in the steps for design, designers can chose the occupancy strategy for the building, where a percentage of the building will be sold to collective investors. At the end of the long term rental lease of 10 years, these units will be made available for adjacent ownership units to buy or rent, with maximum expanded unit size limited to 5 bays. The rental units may be repartitioned to serve multiple units.


Matrix of Evolution +
Application to Different Building Configurations
In Summary, the design methodology aims to impact the design of future housing in 3 dimensions. Shown in the Z axis, the method is flexible and adaptable enough to be applied on the various building configurations, such as the case study subject explored in the research portion of the thesis. 01// Research

In order to propose meaningful ways for flexibility and differentiation of spaces within a housing project, 6 unique types of building configurations were analysed. Since the aim of the research is to explore the possible parameters changes in housing designs, it is essential that these subjects differ vastly from on another to encapsulate a large portion of the entire population whilst not neglecting the depth of the research.

Two forms of analysis were subsequently applied on all 6 subjects.
  • Case study A analyses on the scale of an individual unit — Explore the interchangeability of spaces within the unit to fit the different demographics.
  • Case study B analyses on the scale of the entire building — Determine the reconfiguration possibilities between the units.

    • Existing Housing Models - Case Study Subjects
    Case Study A
    Zooming into case study A, this is one of the example of the analysis that was done on the Po Ming Court case study subject. Since the main aim was to determine the interchangeability of the spaces within the unit, I looked into possible ways for which each unit can be altered to cater to different sets of demographics. Their respective comfort levels were also calculated as shown. The main factor that I was looking out for is the possibility for the unit design to achieve an open plan, as it would constitute the most freedom to alter the spaces within. However, as we can see in this case, it is highly possible for the unit to achieve an open plan, there is still limited change in space that can be done without compromising on comfort levels, or having spaces which have no access open ventilation.
    Case Study A Results
    The overall results done under Case Study A are plotted in the graph below, with the vertical axis of the graph highlighting the possibility and range for interchangeability of spaces. Marked in a red bounding box, the Unit Type C of Pinnacle @ Duxton is the best example where maximum interchangeability of space can be achieved without compromising on comfort levels. The location of the utility core with respect to the rest of the unit has allowed for this unit to emerge as the most flexible design.







    Case Study B
    In Case Study B, the aim was to determine the reconfiguration possibilities between adjacent units, which will provide us with the possible ways for expansion or contraction of a particular unit within the building. The analysis was done with respect to structural system of the building which are dark black lines, as well as the unalterable structures, such as the utility core, highlighted in red. Two main groups of results were observed, where some building configuration only supported for only Non-invasive forms of interventions; while others supported both Invasive and Non-invasive forms of intervention.
    Possibility for Non-invasive Interventions Only
    In the case of Po Ming Court, the building only provided us with the reconfiguration between units to be done through non-invasive interventions, such as the possible addition of external light-weight modules, as indicated in green. The unit shaded in blue represents the new unit foot print, and most of the units are expanded through the addition of the external module between the 2 units, via the kitchen.
    Possibility for Both Invasive & Non-Invasive Interventions
    Looking at the case of Pinnacle @ Duxton, more opportunities for interventions are achievable. In this case, a single unit can be repartitioned to combine with another through the knocking down on non-load bearing walls, although it is a considerably invasive form of intervention. Nonetheless, the characteristic of the building design is that it provide for clear bands of spaces with specific functions – The Service band, Circulation Band and Living Band. This clear demarcation of spaces allow for full flexibility in the living band for reconfigurations to take place.
    Case Study B Results
    The results for case study B hence comprises of 2 main groups. Generally speaking, the buildings in the left group all adopt a shear wall system, which has limited them from achieving greater means of intervention. The overall placement of the unalterable structures in relation to the building structure and unit footprint also impacts the possibility in which the units can be reconfigured to expand or contract in size. In both the case of High Park Residence and Pinnacle @ Duxton, the unalterable structures are arranged nicely in a row, making it easy to alter the living spaces with little restriction limited by the unalterable regions.





    02// Design Studies

    This stage explores 20 different design studies, generated solely from representing a typical housing tower with a specific set of statistical data. The main process consist of 2 steps. The first step is to determine the control towers and the second to represent them based on a specific statistical logic. In this case, 2 sets of data will be represented, namely the actual unit size purchased and the Unit Size equivalent to Price Point.

    Each process starts with a typical housing stack with fixed unit type, location and quantity. The subsequent control towers are then derivatives of the first control tower. Tower 2 shuffles the way in which the units distribute themselves in the vertical direction, and Tower 3 alters the distance between the structural columns, from a one with regular intervals, to one where the intervals decrease incrementally towards the centre.
    Represented Data A - Actual Unit Size Purchased
    The first set of data represented is the actual unit size purchased, calculated based on price of unit divided by the cost per square foot at each storey. When the representation is limited to only the centre units, the overall forms of the tower highlights the extra cost incurred due to the demand in unit location. Exploring the representation in 2 different directions shows the increase in opportunities for more dynamic movement of spaces within the housing tower. Centre units can only expand forwards while the corner units can also expand sideways.
    Represented Data B - Unit Size Equivalent to Price Point
    The second set of data represented is that of the Unit Size Equivalent to Price Points. This is done by setting a fixed price point on all the units, taking reference points as the 1st Quartile, Average, 3rd Quartile and Maximum Unit Price in the tower respectively. It is clear that the upper floors are overvalued as a larger difference in size is observed when the lowermost floor is at the 3rd quartile price than when the uppermost floor is at the price of the 1st quartile.
