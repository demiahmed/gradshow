Kota Tua, Beautiful even when it Floods: Alternate Coastal Futures

Summary

Despite having the potential of being a durable building material, Biorock, a form of calcium carbonate formed by the electro-accumulation of minerals dissolved in seawater, has never been applied on an architectural scale due to its slow accretion process. Until today, it has been mainly used for the restoration of coral reefs which do not maximise the strength of this material. However, in the context of marginalized and informal communities, this natural material accretion can be a sustainable procedure to gain building material resources.

Economic gridlocked and dis-empowered governess in Jakarta create the inevitable reliance on community and alternative source of funding infrastructure development. This thesis plays out the possible narrative of this slow accruing material process in the incrementally submerged coastline of Jakarta. Local communities will be empowered to “grow” a new city for habitation using Biorock. Urban structures with basic communal, housing and aquaculture facilities will be established using Biorock as the main building structure, which would be harvested, transported and assembled over a period of 100 years with processes of accretion and unit aggregation.

An Introduction: Sinking Jakarta

Currently, Jakarta now stands at the threshold of climate change with 40% of the city predicted to be completely submerged by 2050. Instead of finding solutions to save Jakarta from further damage, giant sea walls are simply being erected and plans have even been made to move the capital to Borneo. Currently Jakarta sits around 3m below sea levels and is prone to coastal floods.


The two main causes of coastal floods are 1) sea level rise and 2) land subsidence. For Indonesia, the general rise of sea level in 2030 will reach 15 cm to 18 cm. In 2050, sea level could increase by 25 cm to 30, 40cm to 48 cm in 2080 and finally 50 cm to 60 cm in 2100 . However, sea level rise accounts only a small % of why the capital city may be submerged. The bigger reason behind the sinking capital is attributed to its rapid subsidence. Jakarta is sinking at an average rate of 7.5 cm a year, in some places up to 17 cm.


To combat the issue at hand, the Giant Sea Wall Jakarta was constructed as part of the National Capital Integrated Coastal Development programme (NCICD). It commenced in 2014 and is expected to be materialized by 2025. However, this might not be sustainable in the long run. Firstly, the construction of the outer sea wall in Jakarta Bay will have a profound impact on the fishing communities in North Jakarta. Besides, there have been reports of constant leaking, which leaves the coastal villages of Jakarta constantly flooded. The sea wall has also collapsed several times in 2007 and 2019, giving way to sudden coastal floods that inundated neighborhoods for days.

Design Methodology: Biodesign Fiction

This clearly shows that the sea wall is not a reliable solution in the long run. Not only is it ineffective, but is also detrimental to communities around. This thesis instead seek to explore alternative methods of living through an enduring fabrication of a new living system that will eventually evolve into a sanctuary for communities to live in against unpredictable climatic conditions. The growth of the future living systems will be informed by a more natural and gradual process as opposed to the current curated and engineered means. “Biodesign fiction” which has gained traction over the years would be used as a speculative design approach to envision how biology can be useful in future, provocative scenarios. This thesis will specifically delve into the Biorock process which involves marine electrolysis and its potential to grow limestone building components, exploring how nature and human can design a co- produced space in an urban context.

Biorock

Biorock refers to the cement-like substance formed by electro-accumulation of minerals dissolved in seawater. The process, developed by Wolf Hilbertz, was patented in 1979. It was determined that a low voltage would accrete aragonite, a form of calcium carbonate suitable as a building material. The material is three times harder that Portland concrete, becomes stronger with age and have self-repairing properties. Until today, it has been mainly used for the restoration of coral reefs which do not maximise the strength of this material. Despite the technical breakthrough, little exploration has been done to employ Biorock as a construction material.


There is yet an architectural scale application of this promising material accretion methodology due to its slow accretion process. The dependency of accretion on natural cycles renders this process into a speed that is not typical to current material production standard practice. With low voltage, the Biorock can only grow up to 2 cm per year. However, in the context of marginalized and informal communities, this natural material accretion can be a sustainable procedure to gain building material resources.

Application: Harvest to Assembly

The research simulate over a period of 100 years the process of accretion and unit aggregation based on relationships and rules referenced from water access based on the changing water levels. There will be 4 stages involved namely harvest, transportation, storage and finally assembly before the final Biorock infrastructure is completed. These processes will revolve around the change in tidal conditions.


First, we start with harvesting of the Biorock component, which may happen for about 1-5 years depending on the thickness of accretion that is desired to be achieved.


Wireframes are first harvested and left to accrete Biorock to achieve structural components within substantially deep waters, which would preferably mean the initial coastline of Jakarta. To identify where the components would be harvested, ocean current direction during the spring and monsoon seasons are first determined, as currents are essential in the accretion of Biorock to happen. The intersection of current direction during the two seasons are taken as harvesting nodes.


The components are then transported to various sites to be assembled. Transportation would occur at high tide using boats.


The changes in tidal conditions would cause different levels and areas of submergence daily, monthly and yearly. Daily, tidal heights vary by about 1 metre. During the monsoon, tides may increase by 1-2 metres and up to 5-8 metres during extreme weather events. Due to the constantly changing floodplains, specific routes for the transportation of Biorock will be mapped out. They will first reach storage points, highlighted by the bigger yellow border circle before assembly. Assembly would happen during extremely low tide or no tide conditions for work to be done more efficiently. Over the years, the area for assembly may then expand as more Biorock infrastructure is being harvested and transported inland, creating Biorock islands hubs throughout the city.

Basic Unit Geometry

The modules are 3-Dimensional T shaped of varying heights and lengths. The angle of the T shape may vary which allows either square shaped or diamond shaped enclosures to be formed when joined together.

Basic Unit Texture

The module is broken down into two main parts - it would mainly be composed of a wireframe surface that allows the accretion of Biorock to occur. However, edges where the modules may connect with one another would be made of a metal that would not allow accretion to happen.

Assembly of Components

The Biorock infrastructure would first be build up on site, with the lower levels being much denser in accretion so as to be able to hold up the weight of the infrastructure. Putting lighter components above also make it less labour intensive for assembly. During periods of high tide daily or seasonally, the lower levels of Biorock components would be submerged in water, allowing for more accretion to take place, creating an even stronger foundation for more weight to be added on top later on.


Another important component of the infrastructure would be wooden floors and walls that would subsequently be added to the infrastructure after the wireframes Biorock modules have been aggregated. The walls would be placed in between the axis of the Biorock components connection points as shown in the this simplified exploded axonometric. This creates a variety of room configurations.

Final Massing: Programmes

This would be eventually how a potential Biorock island may look like, with a main core among existing buildings before extending out into the open sea creating a branching network.


As for the programs on site, based on the characteristics of Biorock growth, it would be most ideal to have aquaculture as one of the main program, sea cucumber farming in particular helps with Biorock accretion. Aquaculture in general also benefits from Biorock accretion. Apart from that, housing would be the other main function of the system, with domestic industrial production such as tempe or tikar production integrated within. In the long run, by products such as biofuel or hydrogen gas from Biorock accretion and aquaculture may also be harnessed to power certain community needs. This would create a sustainable system for people to live within the Biorock island.

Hence, the Biorock Island will consist of 3 main areas mainly the housing core, the aquaculture core and transport and storage nodes at the edges.


Within the housing cores, a few tower like multi-use hubs will be established and they may be connected to each other along an elevated platform. The different cores may serve different domestic functions apart from having housing. Within each hub there will also be the presence of public plazas and open decks. The cores will be characterized by the dense, large and high build-up of the modules and would usually surround urban buildings left behind.


The aquaculture core on the other hand would be made up of different types of aquaculture hubs and routes, serving different functions such as shallow water and deep water aquaculture pods, sorting hubs, docks as well as Biorock storage fences. Based on fixed rules on which type of routes connect to which type of hubs, different zones within the aquaculture node will be created. These zones would include shallow water aquaculture housing sea cucumbers and certain shallow water fishes, which would be closer to the housing edge, deep water aquaculture housing oysters and deep water fishes, and finally Biorock storage and docks at the peripheries of the node.






Coastal Futures

The final images show how life may inhabitat the Biorock island with activities of domestic industries and aquaculture, against the remains of the city and against the existing city skyline further South that has been spared from the floods, highlighting how the old, the new and the flooded landscapes may coexist with one another.


While developments in the initial phases would be focused on building infrastructure to create spaces to live and work for communities, the building system may eventually expand to support more complex programs such as water harvesting and even energy production to create a self-sustaining ecosystem to support communities as they continually rebuild cities in the constantly changing floodplains.
