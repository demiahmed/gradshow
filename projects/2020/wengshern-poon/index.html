Re(Polder)-gramming:

The discretization of mammoth infrastructure

Summary

My thesis project touches base on 3 key issues that seek to assess and reflect on the times that we live in.
// [Climate] Crisis. The world is plunging into a state of crisis, yet people do not seem to take these concerns seriously. Sea level rise are about to put millions of people without land, yet we carry on as if this will not happen.
// Poldering. (the diking and draining of coasts) has been argued as a stop-gap measure to rising sea levels, but is it truly a long-lasting solution?
// Capitalism. On a bigger picture, we have to evaluate capitalism’s negative implications on climate change and global warming and think about the post-capitalist model.

This project reimagines a future of living on water whereby infrastructure and life is centred around a renewable material known as biorock, and natural sedimentation replaces the unsustainable practice of land reclamation. The methodology is derived from Landscape Urbanism and Form Axioms, using strategies such as Indexing, Prototyping and Meshing. The utilisation of the Ocean as the site provides a tebula rasa, in which design is not heavily pegged on any particular societal context or human bureaucracy. This leeway provides the possibilities of fictions to be generated, to envision man-made techno-derived society, and also to construct architecture not as a static realm, but as a series of ongoing phases.

Thesis mentors: Eva Castro, Federico Ruberto

About Me

When I was younger, I would dream of theme parks and entire cartoon storylines, which really took up a big part of my childhood imagination. 15 years later, I would finally get to revisit that childhood memory with my M.Arch thesis. I always believed that architecture (or urbanism) reveals the stories and evolution of cultures and peoples, and every piece of built form is no doubt a snapshot of a particular paradigm.

As Bernard Tschumi advocates 'Form follows fiction', I believe that now more than ever, we have to start imagining our futures and painting them in a positive light.

Unlike the polder model in Netherlands, polders in South East Asia are skewed towards authoritarian control, and even the spatial qualities of the polder, such as the water grid, high dykes and zoning plans remove the control individuals have over their land. The damaging effects of poldering leads to the continual destruction of natural habitats and ecosystems. The site of South East Asia is all the more important in reminding us of the unsustainability of this ecology. And pushes the agenda of the alternative spectrum, where infrastructure is public-owned, architecture is accessible for all, and society is reorganised around egalitarian values and altruism.

In projecting a community that was sprouted out from essentially a bottom up process and one that organises and handles chaos and large amounts of information. The fundamental root goes into how management of systems and commodities has shifted from self-organised principles to state-owned, effectively making these systems invisible and out of reach to the masses. Current projections also explicitly tell us “The water will come”. Rising sea levels are inadvertently encroaching on our shores, and as humankind continues to exploit the earth and hide behind their bunkers, the world as we know of today can no longer sustain our future generations.




It is happening here, it is happening now

Hai Phong city was chosen as the site for the prototype. It is located in the Cam-Nam Trieu estuary, where the Cam River meets the Gulf of Tonkin of Vietnam. As a result of its estuarine condition, Hai Phong city is affected by siltation phenomenon, whereby the river continuously deposits sediment at the mouth of the river, resulting in a largely shallow gulf at its harbours. This makes it favourable for sedimentation over traditional land reclamation.

Due to its strategic location in the Northen Vietnam key economic region, Hai Phong city has also experienced rapid urbanization in recent years, with government plans of building polders off the coasts of the existing city, as well as erecting dikes to protect built-up areas, so as to expand and reinforce the city as well as bring in new industries. Amongst its projects of expansion towards the sea, include ports, industrial cities as well as tourism. This escalation of activity proves the relevancy of the fiction.

Biorock Growing Process


Its emancipatory quality arises from its bottom-up fabrication process, which in summary involves dipping metal scaffolding in sea water over a period of time, after which a strong concrete-like material grows on the metal and thickens to become habitable.

The grid of process images below denote the sequential progression of the development of the fiction. In the first few images, the bathymetry and risk areas are processed to determine suitable sites of sedimentation. Breakwaters are then planted at these sites to facilitate the deposition of sediment from the river current to begin the process of sedimentation. As the islands gain volume, a biorock skeleton is constructed, together with residential and productive modules to allow new migrants to set up both industry and life in these new regions.






The culmination of the process results in an amalgamated collage of zones

Discrete Components


The city is built on the fundamental building block of biorock, whereby all planning and architecture has been of a participatory nature by the citizens. Discretization enables technology to make it accessible to the masses, circumventing huge production chains and bureaucracy. Furthermore, the open-source git-hub nature allows many repositories of iterations that otherwise would not allow such a level of diversity of the city.

Below are the amalgamation / construction diagrams of 2 of the high-rise nodes, notably the biorock tower and the residential towers. The biorock tower is agglomerated by a vertical spinal axis whereby biorock storage modules, and factories are attached to the spine. The residential node on the other hand, is agglomerated with 3 distinct modules, 2 homes and 1 aquaponic farm. The connection logic is based off the internal connections present in each module, and also an overarching limit radius of extension.

Modules

The modules consist of residential, circulation, productivity, leisure and biorock manufacturing. As elucidated in the chapter above, these modules are at first metal scaffolding units that are assembled and pieced together. After soaking and dipping them in sea water over a period of time, they will grow biorock along the mesh scaffolding and turn from wireframe to solid. These modules are then ready for inhabitation and for use by the local community.









The common underlying geometry of all these modules come from these basic shapes. With the same dimensions and proportions, these shapes are easily connect-able with each other, and allows for an array of different assemblies. The tetrahedron has a triangular base, and a triangular vertical assemblage, allowing for more hexagonal assemblies that are primarily used in the biorock skeleton as circulation and productivity modules. the square pyramid and the cube are used more for vertical agglomerations, using the square as the connection logic between modules, and the tetrahedron as the connection logic between triangular faces.

The diagram below also illustrates the different variations that are produced with varying time. With the modules soaked for less than a few weeks, they are more porous, and are primarily used for leisure modules, where openness is important. The closed solids, which are resulted from longer dipping times, are used for more private functions, such as residential homes.
