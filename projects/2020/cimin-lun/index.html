Intercoastal Habitation - Koyasuhama

Summary

The thesis lies in the purpose of revitalising the Koyasuhama Fishing Village, a historical coastal fishery that was neglected after the industrial boom of Tokyo Bay. The research investigates the different determinants that underpin and influence Tokyo Bay’s urban landscape, and the influence of the increasing globalization pressures on its coastal communities, more specifically, its small scale coastal fisheries. Furthermore, it will also investigate how a fair trade off between social, environmental and economic interests can be established to put forward a new archetype for aquaculture communities that establishes sustainable collaborative pathways that lead to healthy aquastock. The thesis seeks to renew the decaying urban fabric of the site, encouraging intercoastal regeneration to support thriving ecosystems and habitats. Here, new forms of cohabitation and eco-social equity arise.

About Me

Ci Min holds a Master of Architecture from the Singapore University of Technology and Design. Her thesis and research interests lie in sustainable and ecologically regenerative architecture to facilitate a deeper relationship between nature and mankind.

The State of Forgotten Fisheries

Globally, small-scale fishing has much greater significance than most people realize. These communities are widespread and crucial to employment and food supply, remaining as a dominant form of aquaculture production in the world today. However, they have been historically marginalised and ignored in the development of waterfront cities, with conflicting interests in the competition over the use of water and coastal resources. With the lack of attention and inadequate management of resources towards small-scale fishers, will the world see small-scale fishers slowly losing ground? How can countries better involve these fishing communities in national development plans so that they do not eventually become obsolete?

A Site Inquiry

The thesis studies the site of Tokyo Bay and its ports through the lens of fishing communities. The site chosen is a fishing community along the Irie River, in the Yokohama port. Here, the spatial analysis of the urban fabric is investigated, from the evolution of the inter coast as a small fishing village in the 18th century Edo Period, to the decay of the shoreline of Koyasuhama. Today, it is dotted with dis-used and decaying boats and worksheets that were once used by the fisherfolk. Through the urban analysis, integrating these habitations with issues of concern by the fishermen, the urban site is re-read and characterised by a new fluid process which arises from the conservation of local context, narratives from the stakeholders and socio-economic awareness.

Intercoastal Regeneration to support thriving ecosystems

While the site is regarded as a negative symbol of the decline of a fishing village, it still holds culture and heritage of a disappearing paradigm. The historic urban fabric of the fishing village has provided a unique visual image to the city of Yokohama. The thesis hopes to regenerate the forgotten site in the present state, to preserve these local urban identities, therefore proposing new prototypes to support a more sustainable, intimate and honest architecture.

Emerging forms of Cohabitation

As fishermen have recently begun to return to the fishing village again, they have begun to rebuild the intercoast through the rebuilding of their workshop spaces into the aquatic landscape. These fishermen are either descendants of older fisherfolk from defunct fishing communities in the area or are new fishermen. The project therefore raises the possibility of re-imagining a new community of shared systems, from dwelling to aquaculture and food harvesting to equitable marketplaces.

Harnessing Aquaculture

Aquaculture production domains that produce aquaculture such as seaweed and shellfish are reared and harvested on site, through hatcheries. This is employed through the polyculture vertical farming system, where high yields of shellfish & seaweed can be farmed. The sustainable aquaculture farming system ensures coastal ecology is protected as vertical polyculture is one of the most sustainable forms of food production on the planet, sequestering carbon while rebuilding reef ecosystems. The post-harvest systems are used mainly for the drying of seaweed for commercial sale. Nori that is being harvested needs to be dried and processed in order to be transported to the marketplace.

Thriving Eco-social Communities

The thesis explores possibilities for the site to integrate aquaculture and the public realm which help support fisherfolk living here to have equitable and sustainable enterprises. The Marketplace also increases cultural and economic synergy and serves as local economic driver for the communities living on the site. This improves the livelihood of the local economy and community while enabling visitors to understand the circular economy of aquaculture. Boardwalks that make for immersive cultural experience on the site, for visitors to circulate the site more easily and get more knowledge about the facilities on the site. Facilities to help fisherfolk store their disused boats help to increase water frontage and reduces the amount of wasted space in the water.

Interconnected Systems between Aquaculture and Habitation

Aquaponic treatment facilities are added to the site to enable the fishing community to be self-sufficient. The plants grown here can either be used by the communities in their own consumption or be sold at the marketplace. These aquaponic systems serve as an add-on to the food production on the site and also serves as a resilient system with carbon capturing properties to mitigate the pollution in the river.

Prototypical Aquaculture Model for Fisherfolk

The project and design focuses on a collective model for intercoastal ecology and human habitation to form a symbiotic relationship, through a series of shared and interconnected systems. This therefore forges a new understanding and integration between humans and the waterfront, through an understanding of the complexities of formal/informal and order/disorder of the urban memory of Koyasuhama. In conclusion, while most of the coastal regions of Tokyo Bay have been destroyed by the process of fast urbanisation and waterfront renewal, the thesis aims to evaluate the social and urban memory of this process and propose a new prototype for a thriving and sustainable coastal community.
