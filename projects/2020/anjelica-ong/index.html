Disuse - In use: A sustainable fashion innovation district along Singapore's Arab Street in Kampong Glam

Summary

This thesis aims to project the future of the retail (i.e. decline in physical retail) and fashion industry (i.e. rise in sustainable fashion) on the traditional textile street of Arab Street in the heritage district of Kampong Glam, by adapting it from physical retail spaces in shophouses to a collaborative community-focused fashion innovation hub for a future-ready local sustainable fashion industry in Singapore, focused on fashion research, design, production and supply.

The site chosen is Arab Street in Kampong Glam due as Kampong Glam is one of the four historic districts (Boat Quay, Chinatown, Kampong Glam, Little India) in Singapore. These districts house shophouses that were once largely mixed-use (commercial and residential) areas for the various race groups and, today, have become popular tourist destinations. This project hopes to shift away from Singapore's reliance on rapid rate, business-as-usual construction of entirely new buildings and instead hopes to reevaluate the potential of old buildings, to ensure they stay productive in the future knowledge economy of Singapore. Specifically, in this case, through converting the concentration of textile shops along Arab Street into a sustainable fashion innovation district, in hopes of propelling the nascent creative industries in Singapore through adaptive reuse and infrastructure.

(1) physical retail on the decline

Historically, Kampong Glam used to be an area for Arab immigrants and merchants to settle, many set up shops selling traditional items like textiles and carpets, and some of these shops have been handed down across generations and still exist. Today, Kampong Glam is known for its iconic Sultan Mosque and Haji Lane shopping street. Along Arab Street, one of the vehicular streets in Kampong Glam, it houses about 100 (mostly double-storey) shophouses, with at least 80-90% of them being textile stores occupying physical retail space. However, in tooday's digital age and the trend of a retail landscape shifting towards e-commerce, will the physical retail spaces in the traditional textile retail shops on Arab Street still be relevant in the future? With an inevitable compacting of physical retail in the future, what sort of productive or value-adding spaces and programs could we design for these shophouses?

(2) sustainable fashion on the rise
(3) the relevance of innovation districts in a knowledge-based economy
(4) injecting renewed value in dying spaces through the building of new communities

With a large majority of the shops on Arab Street being various types of textile, fabric and tailoring stores, it makes sense to take a look at the global and local fashion industry; such as the global shift away from consumerism and fast fashion by producers ands consumers alike, and how the fashion industry in Singapore will adapt in response to these new consumption patterns and shift in values of the new generation.

Furthermore, globally, as we move towards a skills-based knowledge economy, there has been a rise and increasing relevance of innovation districts. In land-scarce cities like Hong Kong and Singapore, optimising land use through rethinking low-density development in locations such as heritage districts may be a possible solution. Similar to other innovation districts in Singapore such as one-north (consisting of Fusionopolis, Biopolis, Mediapolis), the large-scale infrastructure provided by an innovation district such as shared facilities, workplaces and proximity to relevant knowledge institutions (e.g. NAFA, LASALLE, SOTA, National Library in the case of Arab Street and the fashion industry) is especially beneficial to support the local creative industries which has not received as much attention and resources as the science and technology fields locally.

By providing the initial infrastructure for the local fashion industry in the site of Arab Street, it hopes to support and grow a new community—built upon the existing community of textile shop owners and tailors—in hopes of reinvigorating the value and purpose of heritage districts in the future of Singapore.
