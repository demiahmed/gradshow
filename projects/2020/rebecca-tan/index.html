Building Blocks for Disassembly

Summary

Buildings today no longer last as long as their ancient predecessors – it is no longer uncommon to witness functional buildings that were constructed just 20 – 30 years ago being demolished to make way for a newer and taller building. Despite being a profession trained to design buildings that last, this cycle of construction and demolition is now mostly met with indifference and nonchalance. This accelerated reconstruction cycle is an unsustainable process -- even with the recycling of debris, the demolition of buildings often still results in significant energy losses and disruption to the city.

This project proposes design for disassembly as an alternative to the common practice of demolishing buildings. Through conceiving buildings as material banks and designing building components to be dismantled and reused, the continual usage of materials over different building life cycles is encouraged, creating a closed and circular material cycle.

By breaking down a building into smaller blocks and attaching tangible value to the everyday surfaces we come into contact with, this project hopes to preserve the longevity of a building in parts and inject circularity and liquidity into the static built environment.

Premise

Though the decay of time is an inevitable outcome for all buildings, buildings today seldom reach their natural deaths. Many structures today are being demolished due to unnatural factors - whether it is due to an unfashionable appearance or insufficient rental space. Through literature research, it was found that the value of buildings is often inextricably linked to outside factors such as the fickle economic climate and changes in public policy. Architecture's dual identity as real estate is often forgotten or ignored. The dichotomy between architecture's immotility and capital's need for liquidity causes a tension that manifests itself as relentless reconstruction. The permanent physicality of a building is no longer as relevant in today's economic environment and it is imperative that we start to design obsolescence as an inevitable part of the building lifecycle.

Design for Disassembly

Typically, buildings are conceived as finished and permanent structures, designed for an average technical and functional lifespan of 50-75 years. Despite their long physical lifespan, most buildings become obsolete in use after a short time. Currently, most buildings are demolished after an average lifespan of 30-40 years because they no longer meet the needs of their users.

Although there has been concerted effort worldwide to deconstruct existing buildings and reuse their building components, most of these efforts have been time-consuming, labour-intensive and economically infeasible. A large portion of such inefficiency is that these deconstruction plans were not incorporated into the initial design of the building.

Design for disassembly is seen as a way to design for the inevitable death of a building. This thesis researched into the current state-of-art of design for disassembly and analyses the strategies used in different case study projects.

System for Disassembly

Traditional linear economy housing model typically ends in the demolition of the building when the land lease ends or when the building becomes obsolete in use. This is due to the irreversible nature of buildings, where different building layers that age at different speeds are permanently connected with each other.

This project breaks down a conventional method of building. A building is broken down into the 4 layers – the structure layer, service layer, space plan layer and skin, Each layer has a predicted lifespan and connecting these layers reversibly will allow parts to be reconfigured during the life span of the building and be replaced at the end of its life.

Building layers are discretised into building components of standardized sizes. It is said that the maximum recommended weight to carry for people is between 15 to 25 kg. The building components are designed to be lightweight, allowing for no more than two people to carry them. The decision to ensure components can be carried reduces the barriers towards disassembly.

Structure

A post-beam system with a cross-laminated timber core is proposed due to its high flexibility. Lightweight glulam sections are assembled and agglomerated to form larger structural components.

The column module is constructed by clustering four smaller sections together. This bundling of elements allows lightweight elements to take up heavier structural loads while maintaining a relatively small footprint. Column modules are then stacked and connected using lap joints and hex bolts. Beam elements are stacked in a manner similar to the Japanese kumo tokyō system to create the ceiling overhead.

All the glulam sections are reversible connected using standardised bolted connections, allowing it to be easily assembled and disassembled using hand held tools. These elements can be easily found in any local hardware store in the event of maintenance. Glulam modules are pre-drilled and passive RFID tagged for the ease of trading and identification.

Services

The service and circulation layer comes in the form of structural plywood floor cassettes that allow mechanical services to run through them. This layer runs the perimeter of the core in the public area of the building to allow for easy maintenance without having the enter residents’ apartments.

Space Plan

Cross-laminated timber floor cassettes are used to fill in the living units.

Skin

Regularly sized façade modules are used according to owner’s preferences.

Implementation

The proposed system is implemented in the context of Japan - the average lifespan of a residential building in Japan is said to be only 25 years due to the rampant ‘scrap and build’ approach. In addition, the timber stocks from the national reforestation effort in Japan after the World War 2 have matured and Japan is currently actively promoting the use of their local timber in the construction industry.

However, due to more affordable imported timber in the 70s, there is a decrease in plantation rates in the Japanese forestry business and this is represented by both the low proportion of domestic timber use and an age gap in timber stocks. Therefore, the increased use of Japanese timber needs to be carried out in a holistic perspective, keeping in mind the possible shortage of timber resources in the future. This includes the promotion of reuse of timber stocks to guarantee the supply of timber resources in the long run. Gotanda in Tokyo was chosen as a site due to its position as a growing start-up community.

Contrary to the more common buy-and-sell manner of development, this project proposes a cooperative housing system that sources and maintains their building components as a collective. First, owners will select the number of polyvalent units they would like to occupy and the number of extensions required. Ownership will come in the form of shares and residents pay for a corresponding proportion of the land lease according to their number of planning units. Subsequently, owners can select the number of building components required. This polyvalent planning method will allow for a large variety of unit configurations to suit different residents’ needs.

The architecture is designed in a manner that allows for a controlled expansion following circulation pathways. As the building expands with an increased number of residents, a highly interconnected development is created where residents can access different spaces through elevated pathways.







Inhabitation

The implementation of the proposed system of disassembly results in a dynamic architecture that can be easily adapted and reconfigured according to varying needs and requirements. As time passes, parts of the building age differently according to where the timber sections were located, creating a rich living environment.
