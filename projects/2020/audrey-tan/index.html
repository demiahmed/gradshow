Urban Deltascapes: An Architectural Prototype for Biomorphic Urbanism in Singapore

Executive Summary

Most of Singapore's coast is lined with seawalls. These 'hard' defensive structures significantly limit a more symbiotic relationship with the sea that can include ecological processes involving both terrestrial and marine environments.

Recognising that climate change and the related rise of sea levels will impact Singapore, this thesis explores an alternative strategy for Singapore — as an urban environment that embraces its relationship to water.

Over the last couple of years, Singapore has increasingly explored urban solutions that include blue-green infrastructure. This thesis acknowledges the trajectory towards 'A City in Nature' as the Nation takes steps towards restoring ecologies through initiatives such as the Active, Beautiful, Clean Waters (ABC Waters). With a renewed interest in nature, the notion of 'Living with Water' is also increasingly explored in Singapore. While efforts like ABC Waters have been appreciable as developments inland have adapted nature-based strategies to manage stormwater, not much has been done along the coastline.

Earmarked for redevelopment as part of the URA Master Plan 2019, the Keppel-Labrador-Bukit Chermin Planning Area presents an ideal testing ground for this thesis. Situated along the coastline and adjacent to the ecologically-diverse Berlayer Creek, this thesis presents an alternative approach to new developments along the Southern Waterfront. Instead of conventional 'hard' defences, this thesis proposes a resilient coast through use of natural systems — to not only protect and restore ecologies, but to develop the city with these natural environments in mind.

This thesis proposes an urban solution that mediates between land and water along the existing ecologically sensitive Berlayer Creek that features one of Singapore's largest remaining mangrove forests. It reinforces the very notion of 'architecture as an extension of nature', as that parallels the build and functions of mangrove trees by taking cues from the surrounding ecology that motivate its form, structure and performative qualities.

About Me

Her research interests include sustainable and ecological architecture. She values the beauty of natural materials and landscapes, and she strives to contribute positively to the built environment in creative ways with sustainability in mind.

The Overarching Issue

Global mean sea level has been rising over the past century at an accelerating rate. Since 1880, the global mean sea level has risen about 8 to 9 inches, with one-third of the contribution coming just from recent decades.

With about one-third of Singapore lying less than 5 metres above sea level, this issue has engendered continuing concern over Singaporeâ€™s coastline.Â 

Today, about 70 to 80 percent of Singaporeâ€™s coastline is protected by hard structures such as seawalls and stone embankments. These structures offer protection from coastal erosion and rising sea levels. However, they leave Singapore devoid of a conscientious relationship with surrounding waters.

Site Selection: Keppel-Labrador-Bukit Chermin Planning Area

The site is chosen due to its ecological character. Located within the site, Berlayer Creek is a key habitat in NParkâ€™s Nature Conservation Masterplan. It consists of a continuous chain of mangroves that holds 40% of Singaporeâ€™s mangrove species. Mangroves are key coastal mitigation tools that are largely effective in attenuating waves, managing storm surges and keeping pace with rising sea levels. They are also key habitats to many marine species in the intertidal ecosystem.

Expanding the Mangrove Plantation

As mangroves thrive in brackish water (a combination of saltwater and freshwater), the site is extremely suitable for propagation of mangroves that would help to increase the resilience of the site. Therefore, as part of the architectural proposal, a new distributary (dotted) will be introduced to areas that experience stormwater inundation and sea level rise. The architectural prototype will be populated along the existing Berlayer Creek and the new distributary, accessed by boardwalks that tiptoe over the mangroves. The mangrove plantation will also be expanded seaward from the mouth of the creek to increase coastal resilience against sea level rise.

Form-Finding

Drawing inspiration from mangroves' structure and their ecological presence, the architectural prototype takes a cylindrical form. The form tapers towards the lower half to connect with and make room for mangroves, performative aspects were incorporated through introduction of an airwell and terraces for rainwater harvesting. Lastly, inclined columns and apertures were introduced for the structure to stand among mangroves and to facilitate wind flow within the building.

Micro-Climate Analysis

Solar irrdadiance studies were conducted to determine the positions of the towers. To increase thermal comfort and shading, proximities between towers decreased and East-West linear arrangements of towers increased.

Computational fluid dynamic (CFD) studies were also conducted to understand how wind flows through a cluster of towers and how improvements can be made. Further simulations were done on a building scale to optimize the amount and extent of openings to achieve a comfortable wind speed of 1.5m/s through the towers.

Structural Analysis

A series of structural studies were done to achieve an optimised structure for the tower. Parameters such as the layers of structure, number of columns and the heights of columns were varied to obtain an optimised structure with very minimal deformation and buckling.Â The same process was applied to other tower variants of heights ranging from 66m to 90m.

Environmental Considerations

The prototype was designed with the intention of adapting to and enhancing the ecological environment. Several performative aspects were explored to allow the prototype to contribute positively to the environment.Â 

Mangroves provide habitat to a wide variety of marine life. The fluctuating behaviour of the waters along coastlines create intertidal zones that house distinct families of organisms. Additionally, with a complex root structure, mangroves are carbon-storage powerhouses. They have the ability to absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and store high amounts of it through a process called carbon sequestration. By sequestering carbon, we minimise carbon emissions and its effects on climate change.Â Â 

To mimick mangroves' root structure, a secondary root system similar to mangroves was developed using a branching algorithm. The distances between the roots are kept in a range between 40cm to 100cm, similar to the architecture of mangroves. The fine roots allow the growth of barnacles and algae that are critical for the intertidal ecosystem at Berlayer Creek and they create shelter for juvenile fishes. They are also able to hold sediments and peat to sequester carbon.

The non-submerged parts of the columns are wrapped with an anioinic resin fabric used in carbon-capturing devices. The resin fabric sleeves can be removed and brought to the laboratories within the development for extraction of carbon once they are filled.Â

Carbon capturing devices will also be placed on the roofs of the towers to maximise the collection of carbon dioxide. These devices developed by Physicist Klaus Lackner can capture carbon a thousand times faster than a natural tree. Captured carbon dioxide can be sold to industries for a variety of industrial and commercial uses â€” including reconversion into clean-burning carbon-based fuels.Â 

In addition, the building utilizes a screw pile foundation that minimizes site disturbance without the need to remove soil from the site. This provides ease of installation within the mangrove plantation.Â 

The terraced atrium is designed for water harvesting. The layers of vegetation filters the water that is collected at the lower levels and can be used in the carbon processing laboratories within the development for extraction of carbon dioxide from the carbon capturing devices. The water can also be released into the water bodies to feed the mangroves, as they grow best in brackish water, or pumped back to service the building. This creates a sustainable cycle of water supply.

It is also important to practise bird-safe architecture especially in this ecologically sensitive environment to prevent birds from crashing into the glass facades. Studies show that the collision zone is up to 75m. As the towers are between 66-90m, they are within the danger zone for birds.

To alleviate that, the glass facade is offset, and a sunshading screen is introduced to reduce the appearance of glass.Â  Â 

The density of the sunshading screen is based on the results of solar irradiance studies on the tower facades. The surfaces of the towers were unrolled to determine the areas that require the most shade. (Fig. 3.5) A similar branching language is applied to the facade design to provide shade to areas exposed to the sun, mainly the East-West regions. Â 

With these environmental qualities, the architecture of the tower parallels the functions of a mangrove tree.

Ecological Heights and Spaces

The intertidal boardwalk is at +6.5m height while the sheltered boardwalk is at +12m height, above the maximum sea level rise of 6m and also accommodating for a high tide of 2.5m. Mangroves grow up to a maximum height of 33m. Hence, the connecting bridges, where communal spaces are located, are positioned above 33m.

A nature trail is paved at the intertidal boardwalk level. The boardwalks are kept thin to minimise the overall footprint of the development. The upper boardwalk level has a more direct access between towers, carbon processing laboratories and water storage facilities are located on this level.

Within a tower, floor areas are divided radially with a continuous planter that encircles the atrium. The lift lobbies are located at the openings of the connecting bridges for convenient access. The connecting bridges houses communal spaces and amenities such as children's playground, rest areas, shops and amphitheatres â€” spaces traditionally found on ground levels of residential developments.


 

The radial distribution of the floor area allows flexible configuration of residential units. Lots can be combined to form larger units. The studio units, 2-bedroom and 3-bedroom lofts can house 2 to 6 people comfortably.

The rooftop spaces will be used to house the carbon capturing devices and will have green spaces for residents to enjoy nature and views.

The intertidal boardwalk submerges at high tide and emerges at low tide, while the sheltered boardwalk remains accessible during both low and high tides, attributing to the experiential quality of the eco-conscious development.

Mangroves have the ability to attenuate waves. Approaching waves reduce in speed and intensity as they pass the mangroves. By extending the mangrove population seaward, mangroves can absorb impact from waves and reduce coastal risks.

Future Development

This thesis strives to inculcate an idea of building with nature and to promote a more symbiotic relationship between land and sea.Â This architectural prototype could be further adapted and applied to other coastal areas of Singapore. Coastal regions with existing mangrove plantations (in green) and urban coastal areas earmarked for redevelopment (in blue) could implement a similar hybrid soft-urban strategy to utilise existing ecologies to manage coastal risks with integrated developments.
