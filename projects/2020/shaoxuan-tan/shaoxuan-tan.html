neo atlantis

living in a post sea-level-rise future

Summary

The coast and ocean is one of the most valuable resources to mankind. We capitalise on them by inventing the concepts of tourism and travelling. However, our current ways of living are not necessarily aligned with tourism development, and are certainly not adapted to live in the waters. The climate crisis in recent years has resulted in the rising of sea level around the world, and many areas, especially tourism destinations, are at-risk of being wiped out in the near future. This thesis explores a possible future where living can go hand in hand with tourism and sea level rise, making use of tools of fiction and infrastructure.

Tourism and the Coast

The presence of tourism is often synonymous with coastal areas. The world is growing richer with the years; the middle-class is growing in many developing countries. With this phenomenon, people possess more wealth and are able to travel around the world more - travel is no longer a luxury but an everyday thing. This has motivated the development of many tourist destinations around the world, and an approximated 80% of such destinations are located in close or immediate proximities of coastlines. The natural beauty of the ocean, aquatic activities and an array of leisure activites are among the many reasons why people step away from their usual hectic lives and into a dreamy wonderland. As such, these destinations have served as critical nodes of economic activity, especially for small developing island nations.
However, while tourism is seemingly beneficial for an areaâ€™s economy, it is often pursued without thought for its implications. As the growth of tourism is often rapid and unplanned, it often causes negative impacts on the environment that it takes root in. In the Caribbeans, such expansion has resulted in issues of deforestation and erosion arising from the need to construct accommodation facilities, while other issues such as water pollution and damage to coral reefs ensue (McElroy and de Albuquerque, 1998). Such a scenario is not limited to the Carribeans, but is widespread across the globe.

Tourism and its Effects

Furthermore, there are also social impacts associated with the influx of tourists into a tourist destination. With a large number of tourists, the locals often find themselves bearing hatred and tension for the tourists. As tourists do not possess any particular sense of identity and belonging to these places, they do not treat these places with the respect needed.
As such, locals may tend to revert to protests as a way of voicing their concerns. This may not necessarily be of use, especially in areas that have become highly dependent on tourism as a contributor to the economy. In the case of Venice, due to its monocultural economy of tourism, the large volumes of tourist is something that cannot be refused. With this, the islands face issues of sinking into the water, and local population has seen a sharp decrease over the years as they are displaced by the sheer number of tourists (D. Bertocchi, F. Visentin, 2019).
With the costs of flights ever increasing, physical travel starts to become limited to a smaller group of people who are able to afford it. As such, drones can come into play in catering to the needs of these people. While the cost of drone travel is high, time-cost savings resulting from faster travels allow these costs to be offset, and allow people to move from point to point within shorter periods of time.
The introduction of blockchain technology in recent years has also allowed for apps and programs to be micro-managed by people themselves. If applied to tourism operations, communities can have better control within their homes.
The Covid-19 pandemic has given popularity to a new form of tourism â€“ virtual tourism, where people from around the world now stay within the comforts of their homes and enjoy travelling as destinations release online versions of their attractions, or have local guides bring virtual people around. This method has proved itself to be effective in reducing the number of people physically travelling, yet main high tourist numbers.
