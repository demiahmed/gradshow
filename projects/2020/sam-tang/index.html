SEA Crossing

Summary

In the shift towards regionalisation in Southeast Asia, an increasing number of cross border projects has taken place in the region, legitimised by ASEAN’s narrative of fostering a regional community. Yet on the ground, these purely economically driven projects result in the further exploitation of the environment and marginalised communities, widening the divide between developed and developing cities. Recognising the sea as a connective body between archipelagaic and maritime Southeast Asia, this project speculates the formation of a transborder region within the Singapore Straits, where social and environmental goals form the foundation for a cross border community between Singapore and Batam.

The Sea

Historically, the sea acted as a fluid body that connected communities and cultures across borders. Coastal settlements along the Straits, although in different countries, had developed cultural similarities due to frequent exchanges. However, increasing industrialisation of the coastline coupled with mass reclamation has resulted in the loss of coastal communities, along with Southeast Asia’s maritime history and transnational linkages. In this context, the industrialised coastline is a direct representative of a thickening line of separation between national territories.


Transnational Straits / Offshore Community

To counter the intensive industrialisation that has taken place along the coastline, the project reclaims the space of the sea via the creation of a transnational region within the Straits - a shared space between the adjoining cities Singapore and Batam - which would facilitate a cross pollination of cultures and exchange, leading up to an eventual building of regional identity. Using the declining oil industry as an opportunity, offshore structures such as oil rigs and platforms are repurposed into structures for hosting urban activities such as housing and trade, providing an alternative future for the offshore industry while de-industrialisation takes place on the coastline.

The Transborder Straits consists of two areas - its main body at the existing Nipah Anchorage Zone, and a series of smaller Islets along the Riau and Singapore Port Limit lines.

Nipah Anchorage Zone

At the mouth of the Straits lies the Nipah Anchorage Zone, an existing transit point for tankers waiting to berth at either Singapore or Riau Ports. Where there is less reliance on oil, the Anchorage zone is freed up to become the core of the Transnational Straits, hosting long term activities such as housing and farming. Combining Batam’s housing demand along with food sustainability within the Straits, Nipah Anchorage’s concentric form breaks up waves in the open sea to create waterspace for aquacultural production and civic use in its outer and inner ring respectively.




Islets

Located along the international shipping route of the Straits, the Islets are a dispersal of Energy Module rigs clustered to form a central Industry Zone, the support core for a ring of repurposed rigs around its perimeter. The Straits, formerly a hub for offshore oil drilling and refinery, re-invents itself into a clean energy hub where the biofuel production and bunkering activities for ships passing through the Straits channel take place.

The Energy Modules, besides producing energy for local consumption, also house international administrative spaces for ships crossing the Straits. Community Park Rigs combine commerce and recreational spaces for craftsmen and residents, while Reserve Park rigs form artificial floating mangroves, which are then replanted along Singapore and Batam's damaged coastline or used as biofuel. Spaces for marine-based lab and research areas are elevated above sea level, while the rig, over time, would provide an artificial structure for the growth of coral reefs and migratory nesting areas.


Rather than a complete rejection of territorial boundaries, differences across the border creates the engine for exchange. By introducing a range of urban activities into the Straits, thereby diversifying its users, we envision a transborder community that would bring together communities from sea and land, a re-establishment of the former coastal heritage of the Straits.
