Life after Coal : Re-imaging Fushun's Post Industrial Landscape

Summary

The use of fossil energy sources including coal have had significant negative impacts on ecosystems and public health worldwide and been a major cause for the current climate crisis. Acting as a universal call to action to protect our planet, the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) have been adopted by all member states in 2015. Goal Number 7 demands a move away from non-renewable to renewable energy sources that are affordable and clean and aims to significantly increase renewable energy in the global energy mix by 2030. As a result, in many parts of the world, coal mines are closing and cities which were built around them are facing economic restructuring. Once at the heart of a large industry, many surface mines have now been abandoned and sometimes reclaimed. 63% of them currently remain unused grassland.

My Thesis explores the relationship between the city and new forms of sustainable development that include energy and food production by proposing the transformation of a large surface coal mine located in Fushun, a city located in China’s Rust Belt region. My project proposes a new housing typology for low-rise residential blocks to transform and adapt organically within a range of slope gradient on the reclaimed site. By integrating blue-green infrastructure throughout the built form, each typical neighbourhood of 2 hectares creates a self-sustaining community of up to 300 residents with mixed-use space for living, working and gathering. Dedicated parcels are developed for the purpose of food and energy production to support the neighbouring cities. A central green space with water body is located at the bottom, not only serving as recreational spaces, but also channeling flows of energy, water, and food within the resilient and sustainable community for 10,000 residents.

Global Energy Overview

Consumption of energy has increased significantly over the past decades. Up till today, fossil energy still plays a vital role in global energy industry, contributing to 85% of the world’s electricity. As coal maintained its position as the largest source of electricity in the world with a 38% share, new mining sites are constructed around the world. However, the use of fossil energy sources including coal have had significant negative impacts on ecosystems and public health worldwide and been a major cause for the current climate crisis, contributing to more than 40% of energy-related carbon dioxide emissions.

Acting as a universal call to action, 17 interlinked goals are put forward by the United Nation to achieve a better and more sustainable future for all. Goal Number 7 aims to significantly increase the use of renewable energy which is clean and affordable in the global energy mix by 2030. These goals were re-interpreted by the Singapore Institute of Architecture in its recent publication “green book” to encourage continuous commitment towards minimizing the negative environmental impacts of buildings through sustainable design.

As a result, there is a gradual shift in the consumption of energy to low-carbon energy resources in the past century. Currently, Countries including Germany, United States and China are leading global renewable energy consumption. China is the largest renewable energy consumer and aims to increase share of renewable energy in its total electricity consumption to 35 percent by 2030.

Post Industrial Landscape

Major surface mines located at northern China are about to close in the next decade. This includes the largest open pit mine which operated since the early 18th century – west open pit mine. As a result, cities which were built around mining sites are facing economic restructuring. This leads to social issue such as economic downturn and demographic changes, each contributing to a more serious situation in the city. This is a global issue which has already been experiencing in other parts of the world. Where coal mines are closing, the cities which were built around them are facing economic restructuring. Surface mines located have been abandoned and sometimes reclaimed. 63% of them currently remain unused grassland.

This thesis looks into such post-industrial landscape, which was reclaimed by filling with water and forest, could be transformed meaningfully, bring positive impact into the built environment and city development by taking advantages of its own industrial and physical conditions on site.

Site Context

Fushun located in Liaoning, north-eastern China, is a satellite city of Shenyang, the capital of Liaoning province, which is 45km away. The city has grown linearly along the Hun river. Where the industrial belt is parallel to the residential belt and separated by the railway system. In each industrial district, the production facility is surrounded by residential districts whereby a secondary transportation network connects all industrial districts together at the outskirts of the city. The Buildings in the city are typically low rise- below 7 stories in height. The typical land parcel size is about 200 by 200m, serving a population of from 1000 to 5000. With a population of about 2 million, the residential usage of electricity only takes up to 11% of city’s energy production. More than 80% of energy is to supply the secondary industry, mainly the mining related transportation and infrastructure.

Located only 1.5km from the city centre. The site, West Open pit mine is one of the largest and earliest operated surface mine in China. Covering an area of 13 square kilometer, this only operated surface mine in the city is about to close by 2030.

Urban Scale Strategy - Grid

On the urban scale, the bridge along the river could be extended into the site as the commercial belt connecting to city. This belt simulates growth within the site parallel to the river, potentially connects to the neighboring mining sites as well as the undeveloped area at the southern region. The city parcel grid extends into the site, forming streets approximately 150m to 200m. Horizontal streets with 200m intervals forms a grid system with a parcel size similar to existing one in the city. An environmental network of eco and aqua system is built up by having a central green and blue spaces located at the bottom of the pit.

Urban Scale Strategy - Parcellation

Solar analysis and slope gradient study were done onto the site. 30% of site area with solar exposure higher than 1000kWh/sqm are perfect for energy production. Such parcels with high slope gradient should be optimized for energy production. Areas with gentle slope gradient and good solar exposure is suitable for dwelling needs. Based on a calculation done previously, distributing 20% the site area to be used for energy production, 30% for residential usage and 20% for eco system, the site can host up to 10,000 residents to be energy, food, and water self-sufficient.

Aggregation

The aggregation pattern is considered allowing the site to be developed in stages. The development will start from the two commercial belts, filling up areas with similar contour and slope gradients, nearest to the existing parcels. At each stage, energy production is sufficient in supporting the current number of residents.

Building Scale Strategy

Comparing to traditional methods, a section illustrated here aims to generate positive impact across the site. A dwelling complex with energy system incorporated can achieve energy, food, and water self-sufficiency. The slope gradient provides an opportunity for water to run off naturally along the contour and being collected in the retention lake at the bottom of the site. After filtering through the central retention lake, it could be pumped back up for residential usages. The slope also provides larger surface area for energy and food production. The excessed could be transported into the neighboring city.

Unit Scale Strategy

Taking a closer look at the slope condition at site, 4 typical slope gradient types are observed. The aim is to take advantage of the existing topography, working with it as much as possible. A typical 3-bedroom unit is designed to fit along site with different gradient, ensuring minimum impact with the current site slope condition. Each unit consists of a front personal garden and back shared green spaces which could be varied in length. This arrangement naturally created a threshold of privacy within the space. The shared walkway at different levels could be accessed by passing through a less private space within the dwelling unit.

System Scale Strategy

Production, ecosystem, and community services are incorporated into the site. A typical parcel size of about 2 hectare is designed based on the 3 services, creating 3 different parcel types. The focus of this project to develop the residential parcel. One typical residential parcel can accommodate up to 300 residents. The units are arranged in a linear manner to reduce heat loss in the wintertime. Production, community and ecosystem services are injected parallel to the housing complex. Building mass slides in accordance with the contour gradient on site, creating a variety of urban ecological space in different sizes between the housing units.

Not only offering both indoor and outdoor farming through green spaces, the housing complex also facilitates with energy production through the installation of solar panels on the roof of the residential blocks. Water is collected at the bottom of each parcel and channeler back to the main central park. A controlled environment could be created by placing a greenhouse roof over the buildings. The units can be combined or split to rearrange into other typical unit types.

Module Scale Strategy

A typical module of urban farm tube is designed along the balcony in addition to the energy system in the house. In summer, it can provide space for vertical food production for the unit, and in winter, it can circulate hot water to provide heat around the house.

Project Potential

Plans of other similar sites along the same stripe varies according to its specific site condition. Along one typical slide, as gets closer to the bottom of the site, more spaces are dedicated for eco and aqua spaces. As strategies developed to resolve the issue from scales large to small, such strategies are replicable and aim to achieve more than what the sustainable development goals are aiming: transforming one abandoned, unused industrial land into a place bringing positive impact not only to the site itself but also the contemporary living environment around it. This proposal aims to serve as a prototype to address similar site conditions not only within the city, but also other post-industrial landscapes around world.
